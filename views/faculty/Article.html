Next-Gen Enterprise Networking Revolution: Cloud Management, SD-WAN Flexibility, and Intent-Based Intelligence

Dr. Deepa Thilak K

Associate Professor, NWC, SRMIST.

Cloud-managed enterprise management with SD-WAN and intent-based networking (IBN) is a modern approach to network administration that simplifies and automates the process of managing a wide area network (WAN).

It combines three key concepts to create a more agile, secure, and efficient network.

  1. Cloud – Managed Networks
  2. Intent – based Networking
  3. SD – WAN

1. Cloud-Managed Networks

Cloud-managed enterprise networks represent a significant shift from traditional on-premises network management to an architecture leveraging cloud-hosted management and control services. In this model, network devices such as routers, switches, and wireless access points are centrally configured and managed through cloud-based platforms. Key drivers for cloud-managed networking include the agility to quickly roll out updates and new features, scalability to support growing and geographically dispersed networks. Administrators gain real-time insights through cloud dashboards and analytics, enabling efficient traffic analysis, device and user activity monitoring, and centralized alerting capabilities. This architectural paradigm reduces the need for extensive on-site network management hardware, permits remote operation, and aligns with IT priorities shifting towards strategic initiatives over routine maintenance.



2.Software-Defined Wide Area Networking (SD-WAN)


SD-WAN is a foundational technology for modern enterprise WANs that decouples the network control from underlying hardware, enabling software-defined management of wide-area network traffic. It provides a centralized orchestration layer that dynamically routes traffic over multiple transport links (MPLS, broadband, LTE, etc.) based on application type, network conditions, and business policies. SD-WAN also enhances security with integrated firewalls, encryption, and segmentation capabilities tailored for cloud and on-premises resources.Enterprises commonly deploy SD-WAN to support cloud adoption, branch office modernization, and digital transformation initiatives. By abstracting the network infrastructure, SD-WAN allows IT teams to centrally enforce policies and gain end-to-end visibility and control across all enterprise locations.

3. Intent-Based Networking

Intent-Based Networking (IBN) is an emerging paradigm that uses high-level business-driven intent to automatically design, implement, and monitor networks. IBN leverages advanced automation, artificial intelligence (AI), and analytics to translate enterprise intent such as security compliance, application performance, or user experience requirements into network configurations without manual coding. IBN continuously assesses network state against the intended policies and takes corrective actions proactively to ensure alignment. By combining AI-driven insights with policy-based network orchestration, intent-based networks enable enterprises to achieve greater operational agility, security enforcement at scale, and simplified network management.

Conclusion

Cloud-managed networking, SD-WAN, and intent-based networking represent a continuum of enterprise network modernization. Cloud-managed networks provide foundational agility and visibility. SD-WAN delivers flexible, secure, and cost-effective WAN connectivity optimized for cloud first architectures. Intent-based networking applies AI-powered automation and policy-driven controls to ensure networks continuously meet evolving business needs.

The Double-Edged Sword of AI in Cybersecurity
Balancing the promise of smarter defence with the risks of AI-powered attacks

Dr. Metilda Florence S

Associate Professor, NWC, SRMIST.

The existing battle between hackers and cybersecurity experts is defined by its ever-changing nature, and the rise of artificial intelligence (AI) has significantly escalated the stakes in the game. Firewalls and antivirus signatures once played a central role in this battle in the past; however, now AI has emerged as the determining force shaping the battle.

Although organizations rapidly implement intelligent systems that can identify and mitigate threats before their manifestation, cybercriminals can also exploit the capabilities of AI to execute more rapid, undetectable, and convincing attacks.

AI for Defence: Smarter Shields Against Complex Threats

The traditional proactive defence model is being transformed by artificial intelligence. Security teams can no longer rely solely on reactive approaches because threats evolve too quickly in the digital age. Next-generation models analyse years’ worth of threat intelligence, user activity, and network logs to identify suspicious behaviour before damage occurs.

A prime example of this is AI-powered intrusion detection, which can predict ransomware attacks by recognizing early indicators in traffic flows.

  1. Scale and Speed: AI systems can detect and respond to threats in seconds.
  2. Enhanced Accuracy: Differentiates real threats from benign anomalies.
  3. Autonomous Response: Automatically isolates compromised systems.

In 2023, a major European financial institution deployed AI-based fraud detection that identified unusual login attempts across thousands of accounts, preventing losses worth millions of euros in real time.

AI for Offense: A Smarter, Darker Playground for Hackers

Unfortunately, attackers are just as eager to harness AI—sometimes even faster than defenders. AI has dramatically changed the cybercriminal playbook:

  • Hyper-Personalized Phishing: AI-crafted messages tailored to individuals.
  • Adaptive Malware: Self-learning malware that evades detection.
  • Deepfake Manipulation: Voice and video impersonation of executives.

In 2024, fraudsters used a deepfake video call of a company’s CFO to trick an employee into transferring $25 million. Even trained staff failed to detect the deception.

Walking the Tightrope: Defence vs. Offense

The dual nature of AI presents a difficult balance. The same technology strengthening cybersecurity also empowers attackers. Organizations must adopt AI responsibly while continuously updating defences to match evolving threats.

Human awareness remains critical. Many attacks still exploit trust, curiosity, or error. Cybersecurity strategies must therefore combine AI-driven automation with training, ethical oversight, and human vigilance.

Conclusion

As AI becomes deeply integrated into cybersecurity, the future may involve AI-vs-AI warfare, where defensive systems counter offensive algorithms in real time. AI itself is neither good nor bad—it is simply a tool. Its impact depends entirely on how it is used.
